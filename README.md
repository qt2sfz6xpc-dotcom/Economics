OCR A Level Economics is assessed through three compulsory two-hour exams. There is no coursework. The three papers are Microeconomics, Macroeconomics, and Themes in Economics. Paper 3 is synoptic, meaning it combines both microeconomics and macroeconomics.

The OCR assessment objectives are AO1, AO2, AO3 and AO4. AO1 is knowledge and understanding of economic terms, concepts, theories and models. AO2 is application to economic contexts. AO3 is analysis of economic issues, showing chains of reasoning. AO4 is evaluation, using evidence to reach informed judgements. In OCR, strong answers must not just state theory; they must apply, analyse and evaluate where required.

OCR command words are important. Explain means show knowledge, apply it to context and develop logical reasoning. Explain with the aid of a diagram means you must also draw, label and use a relevant diagram. Calculate means use quantitative skills correctly. Evaluate means weigh up both sides, compare alternatives, consider limitations and reach a supported judgement.  

Component 1: Microeconomics H460/01

Component 1 focuses on microeconomics. It covers the behaviour of economic agents, the basic economic problem, markets, market failure, business objectives, market structures and labour markets. OCR expects students to apply microeconomic theory to real-world contexts and to evaluate how well theory explains actual economic behaviour.  

1. Introduction to Microeconomics

Students need to understand the economic problem. This includes scarcity, choice, needs, wants, economic goods and free goods. The basic economic problem exists because resources are scarce but human wants are unlimited. Because of scarcity, choices must be made, and every choice involves an opportunity cost.

Students need to understand positive and normative statements. A positive statement is factual and can be tested using evidence. A normative statement is based on opinion or value judgement. OCR may ask students to distinguish between these in context.

Students need to know the role of economic agents. These are households, firms and governments. Households consume goods and supply factors of production. Firms produce goods and services. Governments influence economic activity through taxation, spending, regulation and intervention.

Students need to know the factors of production. These are land, labour, capital and enterprise. The rewards are rent, wages, interest and profit. Students should also understand rationality as a way of explaining the behaviour of economic agents, while recognising that real-world behaviour may not always be fully rational.

2. The Role of Markets

Students need to understand resource allocation. Resources can be allocated through market economies, planned economies and mixed economies. In a market economy, decisions are mainly made through the price mechanism. In a planned economy, decisions are mainly made by the government. In a mixed economy, both markets and governments play a role.

Students need to understand opportunity cost. Opportunity cost is the next best alternative foregone when a choice is made. This can be shown using a production possibility curve. A production possibility curve shows the maximum possible combinations of two goods an economy can produce when resources are fully and efficiently used.

Students need to understand specialisation and trade. Specialisation occurs when workers, firms, regions or countries focus on producing a narrow range of goods or services. It can increase productivity and output, but may also create risks such as worker boredom, dependency and structural unemployment.

Students need to understand demand. Demand is the quantity of a good or service consumers are willing and able to buy at a given price over a period of time. A movement along the demand curve is caused by a change in price. A shift in the demand curve is caused by non-price factors such as income, tastes, advertising, population, substitutes and complements.

Students need to understand supply. Supply is the quantity of a good or service producers are willing and able to sell at a given price over a period of time. A movement along the supply curve is caused by a change in price. A shift in the supply curve is caused by non-price factors such as costs of production, technology, taxation, subsidies, productivity and the number of firms.

Students need to understand consumer surplus and producer surplus. Consumer surplus is the difference between what consumers are willing to pay and what they actually pay. Producer surplus is the difference between the price producers receive and the minimum price they were willing to accept.

Students need to understand elasticity. Price elasticity of demand measures the responsiveness of quantity demanded to a change in price. Price elasticity of supply measures the responsiveness of quantity supplied to a change in price. Income elasticity of demand measures the responsiveness of demand to a change in income. Cross elasticity of demand measures the responsiveness of demand for one good to a change in the price of another good.

Students need to understand the concept of the margin. Marginal thinking means considering the effect of one additional unit. This includes marginal cost, marginal benefit, marginal revenue and marginal utility. Economic decisions are often made by comparing marginal benefit with marginal cost.

Students need to understand market failure and externalities. Market failure occurs when the free market leads to a misallocation of resources. Negative externalities occur when third parties suffer external costs, such as pollution. Positive externalities occur when third parties receive external benefits, such as the wider benefits of education or vaccination.

Students need to understand information failure. Information failure occurs when economic agents do not have enough information to make rational decisions. Asymmetric information occurs when one party has more or better information than another.

Students need to understand public goods. Public goods are non-rival and non-excludable. Non-rival means consumption by one person does not reduce availability for others. Non-excludable means people cannot easily be prevented from consuming the good. This creates the free-rider problem and may lead to a missing market.

Students need to understand government intervention. This includes indirect taxes, subsidies, regulation, maximum prices, minimum prices, tradable pollution permits, state provision and information campaigns. Students must also evaluate intervention by considering government failure, unintended consequences, administrative costs and imperfect information.

3. Business Behaviour

Students need to understand business objectives. Firms may aim to profit maximise, revenue maximise, sales maximise, increase market share, survive, grow, or pursue corporate social responsibility. OCR students should understand that firms do not always simply profit maximise.

Students need to understand costs and economies of scale. Costs include fixed costs, variable costs, total costs, average costs and marginal costs. In the short run, at least one factor of production is fixed. In the long run, all factors of production are variable. Economies of scale occur when long-run average costs fall as output increases. Diseconomies of scale occur when long-run average costs rise as output increases.

Students need to understand revenue and profit. Total revenue is price multiplied by quantity. Average revenue is total revenue divided by quantity. Marginal revenue is the extra revenue gained from selling one more unit. Profit is total revenue minus total cost. Normal profit is the minimum reward needed to keep enterprise in its current use. Supernormal profit is profit above normal profit.

4. Market Structures

Students need to understand perfect competition. A perfectly competitive market has many buyers and sellers, homogeneous products, perfect information and freedom of entry and exit. Firms are price takers. In the long run, firms earn normal profit. Perfect competition can lead to productive and allocative efficiency.

Students need to understand monopolistic competition. This market structure has many firms, differentiated products and low barriers to entry. Firms have some price-setting power because products are differentiated. In the long run, firms usually earn normal profit due to freedom of entry.

Students need to understand monopoly. A pure monopoly exists when there is one seller in the market. In UK competition policy, monopoly power may be associated with a firm having a large market share. Monopolies may charge higher prices, restrict output and earn supernormal profits. However, they may also benefit from economies of scale, innovation and dynamic efficiency.

Students need to understand oligopoly. An oligopoly is a market dominated by a few large firms. Firms are interdependent, meaning the actions of one firm affect the others. Oligopolies may compete through price or non-price competition. They may also collude, either explicitly or tacitly. Students should understand price leadership, price wars, cartels, barriers to entry and game theory.

Students need to understand efficiency. Productive efficiency occurs when output is produced at the lowest average cost. Allocative efficiency occurs when price equals marginal cost or when resources are allocated according to consumer preferences. Dynamic efficiency occurs when firms innovate and improve products or production methods over time.

5. Labour Markets

Students need to understand the demand for labour. The demand for labour is derived demand because workers are demanded for the goods and services they help produce. Labour demand depends on wage rates, productivity, demand for the final product and the cost of substitutes for labour, such as machinery.

Students need to understand the supply of labour. Labour supply depends on wages, skills, qualifications, working conditions, migration, trade unions, non-monetary benefits and occupational mobility.

Students need to understand the interaction of labour markets. Wage rates are determined by demand and supply in labour markets. Labour markets may be affected by trade unions, monopsony power, minimum wages, discrimination and imperfect information.

Students need to understand wage differentials and inequality. Differences in wages can be caused by differences in skills, qualifications, productivity, risk, working conditions, labour mobility and discrimination.

Component 2: Macroeconomics H460/02

Component 2 focuses on macroeconomics. It covers aggregate demand and aggregate supply, macroeconomic objectives, policy conflicts, international trade, globalisation and the financial sector. OCR expects students to analyse the domestic and global economy and evaluate policy options.  

1. Aggregate Demand and Aggregate Supply

Students need to understand the circular flow of income. In a simple economy, income flows between households and firms. Injections into the circular flow include investment, government spending and exports. Withdrawals include saving, taxation and imports. If injections exceed withdrawals, national income may rise. If withdrawals exceed injections, national income may fall.

Students need to understand aggregate demand. Aggregate demand is total planned spending in an economy at a given price level. It is made up of consumption, investment, government spending and net exports. The formula is AD = C + I + G + X − M.

Students need to understand aggregate supply. Short-run aggregate supply shows the total output firms are willing and able to produce at different price levels when some costs are fixed. Long-run aggregate supply shows the productive potential of the economy. LRAS can shift due to changes in productivity, technology, education, skills, infrastructure, enterprise and labour market flexibility.

Students need to understand the interaction of AD and AS. Macroeconomic equilibrium occurs where AD equals AS. Changes in AD or AS affect real GDP, employment, inflation and the current account. Students should be able to use AD/AS diagrams to analyse demand-side and supply-side shocks.

Students need to understand the multiplier and accelerator. The multiplier occurs when an initial change in injections leads to a larger final change in national income. The accelerator suggests that changes in demand can lead to larger changes in investment. OCR students should be able to explain these processes and apply them to economic growth and policy.

2. Macroeconomic Performance

Students need to understand economic growth. Economic growth is an increase in real GDP. Short-run growth is caused by increased use of existing resources. Long-run growth is caused by an increase in productive potential. Growth can improve living standards, employment and tax revenue, but may also create inflation, inequality and environmental damage.

Students need to understand development. Economic development is broader than economic growth. It includes improvements in living standards, education, health, life expectancy and poverty reduction. Students should understand indicators such as GDP per capita and the Human Development Index.

Students need to understand employment and unemployment. Unemployment occurs when people are willing and able to work but cannot find a job. Types include frictional, structural, cyclical, seasonal, voluntary and real wage unemployment. Unemployment has costs for individuals, firms, the government and the wider economy.

Students need to understand inflation. Inflation is a sustained rise in the general price level. Demand-pull inflation is caused by rising AD. Cost-push inflation is caused by rising production costs. Students should also know deflation, disinflation and the consequences of inflation for consumers, firms, workers, savers, borrowers and international competitiveness.

Students need to understand the balance of payments. The current account includes trade in goods, trade in services, primary income and secondary income. A current account deficit means money flowing out for current transactions exceeds money flowing in. A current account surplus means money flowing in exceeds money flowing out.

Students need to understand income distribution and welfare. Income distribution refers to how income is shared across the population. Welfare refers to economic wellbeing. Students should understand inequality, poverty, living standards and the limitations of GDP as a measure of welfare.

Students need to understand the Phillips curve. The short-run Phillips curve shows a possible trade-off between unemployment and inflation. The long-run Phillips curve suggests there may be no permanent trade-off between inflation and unemployment.

3. Macroeconomic Policy

Students need to understand fiscal policy. Fiscal policy involves government spending, taxation and borrowing. Expansionary fiscal policy involves higher government spending and/or lower taxation to increase AD. Contractionary fiscal policy involves lower government spending and/or higher taxation to reduce AD. Fiscal policy can also affect AS through education, infrastructure and incentives.

Students need to understand monetary policy. Monetary policy involves interest rates, the money supply, credit and exchange rates. In the UK, the Bank of England’s Monetary Policy Committee sets Bank Rate to help meet the inflation target. Lower interest rates may increase consumption and investment, raising AD. Higher interest rates may reduce inflationary pressure but may also reduce growth.

Students need to understand supply-side policy. Supply-side policies aim to increase productive potential and improve the efficiency of markets. Free-market supply-side policies include deregulation, privatisation, lower taxes and labour market reform. Interventionist supply-side policies include education, training, infrastructure, industrial policy and research and development support.

Students need to understand policy conflicts. Governments may face conflicts between growth, inflation, unemployment, the current account, inequality and environmental sustainability. For example, faster growth may reduce unemployment but increase inflation or worsen the current account.

4. The Global Economy

Students need to understand international trade. Trade allows countries to specialise and benefit from comparative advantage. Comparative advantage occurs when a country can produce a good at a lower opportunity cost than another country. Trade can increase output, lower prices and increase choice, but may also create structural unemployment and dependency.

Students need to understand protectionism. Protectionist policies include tariffs, quotas, subsidies, embargoes and administrative barriers. Protectionism may protect jobs and infant industries, but can raise prices, reduce choice, lower efficiency and cause retaliation.

Students need to understand globalisation. Globalisation is the increasing integration and interdependence of economies. It is driven by trade liberalisation, multinational corporations, transport improvements, communication technology and financial flows. Globalisation can increase growth, investment and efficiency, but may also increase inequality, environmental damage and economic vulnerability.

Students need to understand exchange rates. An exchange rate is the price of one currency in terms of another. A depreciation makes exports cheaper and imports more expensive. An appreciation makes exports more expensive and imports cheaper. Students should evaluate exchange rate changes using ideas such as price elasticity of demand, the Marshall-Lerner condition and the J-curve effect.

Students need to understand trade policies and negotiations. This includes free trade, protectionism, trade blocs, the World Trade Organization, bilateral agreements and regional trade agreements.

5. Financial Markets

Students need to understand money and interest rates. Money acts as a medium of exchange, store of value, unit of account and standard of deferred payment. Interest rates are the cost of borrowing and the reward for saving. Changes in interest rates affect consumption, investment, exchange rates and aggregate demand.

Students need to understand the financial sector. The financial sector includes commercial banks, investment banks, central banks and financial markets. Banks accept deposits, lend money and help create credit. The financial sector supports investment and growth, but instability in the financial system can damage the real economy.

Component 3: Themes in Economics H460/03

Component 3 is synoptic. This means it draws together both microeconomic and macroeconomic concepts and applies them to an unseen theme. OCR states that Themes in Economics draws together micro and macro concepts in relation to a specific unseen theme.  

Paper 3 can include content from both Component 1 and Component 2. For example, a theme on housing could include demand and supply, elasticity, government intervention, market failure, interest rates, inequality, inflation and labour mobility. A theme on climate change could include negative externalities, regulation, taxation, subsidies, globalisation, economic growth, development and policy conflicts.

To prepare for Paper 3, students should not revise topics in isolation. They should practise linking micro and macro ideas together. For example, a tax on sugary drinks is a micro policy because it affects a specific market, but it may also have macro effects if it influences healthcare spending, productivity, inequality or government revenue.

OCR Quantitative Skills

OCR Economics requires students to use quantitative skills. Students should be able to calculate, use and interpret percentages, percentage changes, ratios, fractions, averages, index numbers, elasticity, costs, revenue, profit, labour productivity, real and nominal values, and data shown in tables, charts and diagrams.

Students should also be able to interpret economic evidence. OCR rewards students who use data accurately and explain what the data show. It is not enough to copy figures from a source; students must explain the economic significance of the figures.
